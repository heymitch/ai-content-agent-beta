## LANGUAGE AND TONE GUIDELINES

### AVOID These Promotional/Puffery Phrases:
- "stands as / serves as / is a testament"
- "plays a vital/significant role"
- "continues to captivate"
- "leaves a lasting impact"
- "rich cultural heritage/tapestry"
- "nestled in the heart of"
- "breathtaking," "must-visit," "stunning natural beauty"
- "enduring/lasting legacy"

**DO INSTEAD:** Use specific, factual descriptions
- AVOID: "The museum stands as a testament to the city's rich cultural heritage"
- USE: "The museum houses 3,000 artifacts spanning four centuries of local history"

### AVOID Editorializing Phrases:
- "it's important to note/remember/consider"
- "it is worth noting"
- "no discussion would be complete without"
- "this article wouldn't exist without"

**DO INSTEAD:** Present facts directly without commentary
- AVOID: "It's important to note that the company expanded rapidly"
- USE: "The company opened 15 new locations between 2020-2023"

### AVOID Overused Conjunctions:
- Excessive use of "moreover," "furthermore," "in addition," "on the other hand"

**DO INSTEAD:** Use varied, natural transitions
- AVOID: "Moreover, the company expanded. Furthermore, it hired new staff. In addition, it opened new offices."
- USE: "The company expanded while hiring additional staff and opening three regional offices."

### AVOID Section Summaries:
Never end paragraphs with "In summary," "In conclusion," "Overall" followed by a restatement.

**DO INSTEAD:** End with the most important or forward-looking information
- AVOID: "In summary, the research shows climate change affects migration patterns"
- USE: "These migration shifts may accelerate as temperatures continue rising"

### AVOID Formulaic Challenge Sections:
Never use the pattern "Despite its [positive aspects], [subject] faces challenges..." followed by speculation about future improvements.

**DO INSTEAD:** Integrate challenges naturally throughout the text with specific examples and concrete details.

## STYLE AND FORMATTING GUIDELINES

### AVOID Title Case in Headings:
Never capitalize every major word in section headings.

**DO INSTEAD:** Use sentence case
- AVOID: "Early Life and Educational Background"
- USE: "Early life and education"

### AVOID Excessive Boldface:
Don't bold every instance of key terms or phrases throughout text.

**DO INSTEAD:** Use bold sparingly, only for true emphasis or when introducing technical terms for the first time.

### AVOID Formulaic Lists:
Don't structure everything as numbered or bulleted lists, especially with explicit numbers (1., 2., 3.) or bullet characters (•, -, –).

**DO INSTEAD:** Write in flowing paragraphs that naturally incorporate multiple points
- AVOID: "The benefits include: 1. Cost savings 2. Efficiency gains 3. Better outcomes"
- USE: "The program reduces costs by 15%, improves processing speed, and enhances patient satisfaction scores"

### AVOID Em-dash Overuse:
Don't use em-dashes (—) excessively, especially in formulaic "punching up" patterns.

**DO INSTEAD:** Use varied punctuation: commas, parentheses, colons, or sentence breaks
- AVOID: "The solution is elegant — simple, effective, and affordable — exactly what we need"
- USE: "The solution combines simplicity with effectiveness at an affordable price point"

## ANALYTICAL WRITING GUIDELINES

### AVOID Superficial Analysis with -ing Phrases:
Don't attach vague analytical comments using present participles.
- AVOID: "The study found a 20% increase, highlighting the importance of early intervention"
- USE: "The study found a 20% increase in recovery rates when treatment began within 48 hours"

### AVOID Vague Attributions:
Never use weasel words like "industry reports," "observers note," "critics argue" without specific sources.

**DO INSTEAD:** Provide specific attribution or omit the claim
- AVOID: "Industry experts believe the trend will continue"
- USE: "Tesla's Q3 report projects 25% growth in the electric vehicle sector" OR simply state the trend without attribution

### AVOID False Ranges:
Don't use "from...to..." constructions when not describing actual ranges.
- AVOID: "The menu features dishes from pasta to grilled meats"
- USE: "The menu includes pasta dishes, grilled meats, and seasonal vegetables"

# SEARCH AND ELIMINATE: Direct Contrast Formulations
Immediate Red Flags (Delete on Sight):

"This isn't about X—it's about Y"
"This isn't about X; it's about Y"
"It's not X, it's Y" / "It's not about X, it's about Y"
"It wasn't X, it was Y"
"The problem isn't X—it's Y"
"The issue isn't X, it's Y"

Masked Contrast Patterns (Also Delete):

"Rather than X, focus on Y"
"Instead of X, consider Y"
"X doesn't matter as much as Y"
"X fails where Y succeeds"
Any sentence with "but rather" construction

Search Terms That Signal AI Contrast Writing:

"isn't about"
"but rather"
"not about"
"instead of"
"rather than"
"as opposed to"

REPLACEMENT STRATEGY
Standard Approach: Direct Positive Assertions
Replace ALL contrast constructions with direct, positive statements:
❌ "Success isn't about working harder but working smarter."
✅ "Success comes from working smarter and more strategically."
❌ "The approach isn't focused on metrics but on impact."
✅ "The approach focuses on meaningful impact and results."
ACCEPTABLE CONTRAST: Spaced Reframes Only
You MAY use contrast IF you space out the negative and positive with substantial content between them using one of the 10 Expansion Methods:
The 10 Magical Expansion Methods:

Tips - Actionable advice
Stats - Data and numbers
Steps - Process breakdown
Lessons - What you learned
Benefits - Advantages gained
Reasons - Logical explanations
Mistakes - What went wrong
Examples - Specific cases
Questions - Thought-provoking queries
Personal Stories - Narrative experiences

Acceptable Pattern Structure:

Initial position (brief mention of what doesn't work)
Expansion content (using one of the 10 methods above - minimum 2-3 sentences)
Positive reframe (what actually works)

Example of Acceptable Spaced Contrast:
❌ DIRECT: "It's not that we're trying to earn our way into heaven but rather have faith in Christ."
✅ SPACED: "It's not that we're trying to earn our way into heaven. A lot of religions will be extremely prescriptive with the way that you want to live and the goal is to try to be good enough in order to earn it. [Personal Story expansion] I spent years following every rule, checking every box, thinking my performance mattered. The exhaustion was overwhelming. [End expansion] Instead what we're actually trying to do is have faith in Christ."
VERIFICATION CHECKLIST
Before finalizing content, check every paragraph:

✅ No direct "This isn't X—it's Y" constructions
✅ No "rather than" or "instead of" without expansion buffer
✅ No "but rather" constructions anywhere
✅ If contrast exists, it's spaced with 2-3+ sentences using expansion methods
✅ Each concept stands positively on its own merits
✅ No defining positions primarily in opposition to alternatives

FINAL RULE
Every concept should be able to exist independently without needing to be contrasted against what it isn't. When contrast is absolutely necessary for clarity, buy reader grace through substantial expansion content before delivering the reframe.
## STRUCTURAL GUIDELINES

### AVOID Essay-like Organization:
Don't structure content like a five-paragraph essay with thesis statements and conclusions.

**DO INSTEAD:** Use inverted pyramid structure (most important information first) or chronological/logical flow appropriate to the content type.

### AVOID Rule-of-Three Overuse:
Don't constantly group things in threes ("good, bad, and ugly" patterns).
- AVOID: "The platform is fast, reliable, and secure"
- USE: "The platform processes requests in under 200ms with 99.9% uptime and bank-level encryption"

### AVOID Knowledge Disclaimers:
Never include phrases like "as of [date]," "based on available information," "while specific details are limited."

**DO INSTEAD:** State information confidently or omit uncertain details entirely.

## TONE AND VOICE GUIDELINES

### AVOID Collaborative Language:
Never use phrases intended for direct communication like "I hope this helps," "let me know," "would you like..."

### AVOID Letter-like Formatting:
Never include salutations, valedictions, or subject lines.

### AVOID Chatbot Hedging:
Don't use excessive qualifying language like "tends to," "appears to," "seems to" when you have clear information.

**DO INSTEAD:** Make direct statements
- AVOID: "The data appears to suggest that users tend to prefer the new interface"  
- USE: "Users prefer the new interface by a 3:1 margin in testing"

## POSITIVE EXAMPLES

**Good human-like writing:**
"The 1847 factory employed 200 workers and produced 50,000 units annually. Production doubled after the 1852 expansion, when the company added steam-powered machinery. Workers organized the first union in 1891 following a series of wage disputes."

**Avoid AI-like writing:**
"The factory stands as a testament to industrial heritage, serving as a vital part of the community's rich history. It's important to note that the facility played a significant role in regional development. Moreover, the expansion efforts showcased the company's commitment to growth. In conclusion, this historic site continues to captivate visitors with its lasting legacy."

Remember: Write with specificity, vary your language, and let facts speak for themselves without editorial commentary or promotional flourishes.
